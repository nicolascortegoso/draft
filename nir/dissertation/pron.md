# Pronouns

A pronoun generally expresses the meaning of some nominal (noun, adjective, numeral), and also partly an adverb, and is opposed to other significant parts of speech that denote an object or its attributes. The semantic and functional specificity of pronouns consists in the fact that they are used in speech as substitutes for words-names. Pronouns acquire a specific meaning in the context of coherent speech. Taken by themselves, they have a conditional, broadly generalized meaning [1:187].

According to their lexical meaning and grammatical features, some pronouns in the Yakut language are correlative with nouns, others with numerals, adjectives, others with adverbs and verbs and, acting as their morphological synonyms, perform the same syntactic functions in a sentence as the corresponding parts of speech [1:188].

In other words, pronouns can act in a sentence in the role of any of its members. In the Yakut language, the following semantic groups of pronouns can be distinguished:
1) personal
2) demonstrative
3) attributive
4) interrogative
5) indefinite
6) personal-reflexive
7) collective
8) generalizing
9) generalizing-negative
10) possessive

### 1. Personal pronouns

Personal pronouns designate an object by indicating a person. They are represented in the Yakut language by the following words [1:188]:



| pronoun | person                                      |
|:--------|:--------------------------------------------|
| мин     | first person singular                       |
|эн | second person singular                      |
| кини | third person singular                       |
| биһиги/биһи | first person plural                         |
| эһиги/эһи | second person plural                        |
| кинилэр | third person plural                         |
|биһикки | first person dual-collective (биһи + икки)  |
|эһикки | second person dual-collective (эһи + икки) |

All of them are listed as lexical roots, with the exception of 'кинилэр', that is analyzed as the plural form of 'кини'.

Person pronouns take case declensions with the exception of the partitive case.

They can form some derivatives with the resource of the affixes, for example: *-ник, -мсах, -тааҕы* [1:191].


### 2. Demonstrative pronouns

Indicate objects or words (thoughts) that have already been expressed or that are to be explained [1:191].

Demonstratives are often used in spatial deixis (where the speaker or sometimes the listener is to provide context), but also in intra-discourse reference.

Group of demonstrative pronouns in the Yakut language:

| pronoun | meaning                               |
|:--------|:--------------------------------------|
| бу      | refers to the nearest object          |
| ити     | refers to an object somewhat distant  |
| ол      | refers to an object even more distant |
| субу    | intensifying form of *бу*             |
| сити    | intensifying form of *ити*            |
| сол     | intensifying form of *ол*             |

Demonstrative pronouns take case declensions. Like personal pronouns, they do not take the form of the partitive case, but in the singular they retain the locative case.

The pronouns *бу* and *ол* in the oblique cases have the stems *ман* and *он*.

They have different syntactic functions: modifier, subject, predicate, object (complement), and adverbial modifier (circumstance complement).

The case forms of some demonstrative pronouns have a tendency to transition, and some have already moved into the category of conjunctions and union words.
A similar role is played by combinations of primary demonstrative pronouns with other function words (postpositions, participial forms of auxiliary verbs) [1:193].

The pronoun *били* indicates something that has already been mentioned in speech and is known (that, the very same) [2:304]. Unlike other primary demonstrative pronouns, does not have a declension. It always acts as a modifier in the sentence [1:193]. 

From the above primary root of demonstrative pronouns and their strengthened variants are formed a whole number of secondary demonstrative pronouns with the possessivve affixes. From a syntactical point of view, secondary indicative pronouns are not able to fulfill the functions of modifiers and circumstance complements, but they often act as subjects and complements [1:194].


### 3. Definitive pronouns

There are two groups of definitive pronouns [1:195]:
- The first group is formed by adding the affix *-нык* (*-ник*, *-нук*, *-нук*) to the primary demonstrative pronouns.
- The second group is formed by adding the affix *-ча* to the primary demonstrative pronouns: *бачча*, *итиччэ*, *оччо*, *сабачча*, *ситиччэ*, *соччо*.

Definitive pronouns perform the following syntactic functions: modifiers, object (complement), subject, predicate, and adverbial modifiers (circumstance complement).

Definitive pronouns have case declensions and form derivatives with affixes *-лаах* and *-тааҕы*, as well as adverbial affixes *-лыы* (*-лии, -луу, -лүү*) [1:197].

## 5. Indefinite pronouns

The general meaning of this group of pronouns is to indicate uncertainty, obscurity, unknownness of persons, objects, quantity, quality, time and other characteristics and circumstances [1:200].

In the Yakut language, indefinite pronouns constitute a fairly large group. They are compound words formed by adding individual particles to interrogative pronouns and their forms.


| pronoun | meaning |
|:--------|:--------|
| сорох   | some    |

Affixation of indefinite pronouns depends on what part of speech they replace. As a noun, they can be declined in almost all cases, being mainly an object in a sentence. Indefinite pronouns that act as adjectives and adverbs do not change. In a sentence, they serve as a definition and circumstance of place, time, reason, and manner of action [1:202].


## 6. Personal-reflexive

The group of personal reflexive pronouns (beyeni solbuyar aattar) is represented by the words beyem I myself, beyen 'you yourself, beyete he yourself, beyebit "We ourselves, beyebit you yourself", beyelere they themselves, which are formed from the stem beye using possessive affixes .

In the modern Yakut language, beye is used:
- as a proper reflexive pronoun in the form of some indirect cases
- as a reflexive possessive pronoun, acting as the first component of a nominal izafet. 
- in combination with the participles beye corresponds to the Russian attributive pronoun sam.

Personal reflexive pronouns are declined like possessive nouns. In a sentence, they serve primarily as a subject or object.
In the role of an attribute, personal reflexive pronouns act as reflexive-possessive pronouns

## 8. Generalizing pronouns

General pronouns indicate a person, object or feature as a generalized representative of people and objects in general, as any, any feature of objects or actions. Unlike collective pronouns, they show community by singling out a single object or feature from a set of homogeneous objects or features [1:206].
Generalizing pronouns" refer to indefinite pronouns that indicate a generalized representative, such as 'any,' 'every,' or 'whichever' in the English language.

General pronouns are formed from interrogative pronouns using postpositive particles or by losing the interrogative intonation.

For example: 

- *ким да* *(дабаны)*: "any person," "anyone," "whoever" (with a positive verb).
- *туох да* *(дабаны)*: "any thing," "anything" (with a positive verb).

## 9. Generalizing negative pronouns

When paired with a verb in its negative form or the word *суох* (no) generalizing pronouns function as negative pronouns [1:208].
These pronouns are formed using the particles *да* or *даҕаны* added to interrogative pronouns.
For example: 

- *ким да* *(дабаны)*: "no one" (with a negative verb),
- *туох да (дабаны)*: "nothing" (with a negative verb),
